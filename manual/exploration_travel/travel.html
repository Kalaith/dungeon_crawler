Travel System

Overview

It's time to break out the adventuring gear and head for the signpost. As you travel through towns, you'll see posts pointing the way to one destination or another. Typically, there are numerous posts in a single town, each leading to a different destination. Among all the posts, there are two basic differences:

Harbours

Signposts at the water's edge give you the chance to secure passage aboard ships traveling to various locales. Since ships come and go, the destinations found at the seaports change daily. A word with the Harbour Master can offer you an expanding look at the harbor's traffic. When you step up to a port's signpost, you'll receive the Port Options.

Path/Road

Signposts at a village's edge note the way of roads and paths to other towns and follow the general layout of the world. For example, signposts to the east generally lead to eastern destinations. When you opt to travel on foot, you'll enter the Traveling Options.

Packing Your Bags

Before your characters head out of town, spend a few minutes with them. It may be several hours (maybe even several days) before they see a town or a merchant again. Make certain they're prepared for the trip:

Pre-Travel Checklist:

  • Rations and Water: Check the amount each character carries. Buy more if needed. Pizza places do not deliver to the middle of dangerous territory.
  • Hunger and Thirst: Fill bellies and quench thirst before leaving. Otherwise, you may be forced to stop mid-route when a character complains of hunger pangs.
  • Equipment Condition: Are all weapons and armor whole? If not, visit a smith to make the necessary repairs.
  • Rest Status: Are your characters well rested? If not, catch some shut eye.
  • Health Status: Should you make a last-minute trip to a healer to treat disease, wounds, or poison?
  • Supplies: Do you have a good selection of herbs, potions, or other things you might need? Don't ignore adventuring gear.
  • Party Leader: Put your best character forward. If traveling in wilderness, characters with strong Nature skills are best to lead. Characters traveling by boat benefit from Survival skills.

Leaving Town Via Ship

About Ships

Many settlements grew around bodies of water. These waterways allowed for easy passage of goods and generally offered settlers fertile land. Harbor towns generally service ships that travel along the coast to the next settlement. The vessels travel to sizable cities within a short range of their home port. In large cities, there are often many destinations as ships come and go from all areas.

Ship Speeds

  • Smallest ships: ~30 miles per day
  • Large trading ships: Up to 120 miles per day

Harbour Options

Book a Passage

Select Book a Passage to make your reservations. The system will show you a list of ships at anchor in the harbor and their destinations. This list changes as ships depart and new ships come in (see the Harbour Master for a complete schedule), so check back if nothing available suits you.

Ship Quality Matters:

  • Expensive ships: Generally travel fastest
  • Luxurious ships: Offer more restful (and healing) trip
  • Small leaky skiffs: May offer free passage but poor conditions
  • Longships: Characters expected to man the oars, won't get much rest during voyage
  • When cash is low: Any old ship will do

Should you accept the captain's offer, they'll note the departure time before returning to their ship with your fare. You may board the ship now or at any time before it leaves port. If you have a few hours, you can head into town. However, keep your eye on the time and return before your ship's set to leave. The captain will not hesitate to leave without you.

Harbour Master's Office

For a small gratuity, the Harbour Master has a world of information for the roaming party. They'll gladly tell you about all ships leaving the harbor and even those that are expected (within a reasonable time frame anyway). For parties traveling to obscure destinations that are serviced rarely, the Harbour Master's knowledge is essential.

Go On Board

If you've booked a passage on a vessel, select Go On Board when you're ready to leave town. After selecting this option, you'll spend your time chatting with other shipmates or looking for fish over the sides of the ship. Once you board a ship, you are on board until it reaches its final destination and have no influence on the ship's course.

Leave Harbour

If your ship hasn't come in, or if it's not going to be leaving for a while, select Leave Harbour to return to town.

Heading Out On Foot

Dust off the backpack and put on your best leather boots... your characters are out for a bit of that heel-to-toe shuffle. Anywhere your party finds a land-locked signpost, this type of adventure is available. Just step up to the signpost to discover where the road or path heads. Keep in mind that each city typically has more than one signpost, so checking around, you'll find roads to more than one destination.

Travel Options

Description

Move the pointer to any area on the map and click with the left mouse button. You'll see a description of the area, its peoples, and its climate. While your party can't travel to any old location you click on (remember, you're heading out on a specific road to a specific place), this feature allows you to see the geography and demographics quickly. Reviewing this map can be very useful in planning your routes across the world or for the curious adventurer finding out about many towns and cities. However, sometimes dirt tracks or wilderness routes will not be so obvious. It may be necessary to check the possible destinations by actually investigating a settlement's journey points (signposts) to find these hidden routes.

Travelling Options

Click the right mouse button to show you the paths available from this signpost's road. You will see your current location highlighted with a pulsing blue circle, and the targeted destination will be highlighted in red. If the destination is okay with your party, select it and you're off. You may also select "BACK" to return to the town you're currently in. If you decide to travel, your party's progress will be shown on the map and will deposit you safely at your destination... unless your party experiences one of the hazards of traveling.

Travelling Time & Movement Points

Travel is measured in Movement Points (MP). Each day, your party has a pool of MP based on their travel mode. Moving across the map consumes these points based on the terrain.

Travel Type Daily MP Pool Conditions
Normal Road Travel 30 MP Decent conditions, maintained roads (1 MP per mile)
Poor Conditions 15-20 MP Bad season, poor road conditions
Wilderness Travel 15 MP Hidden and overgrown routes, tremendously difficult (2 MP per mile)
Ship (Coastal) 30 MP Standard coastal travel
Ship (Open Sea) Up to 120 MP With favorable winds

Travel Strategy

Choosing Transport

  • Ship Advantages: Faster travel (up to 120 mi/day), less exposure to wilderness hazards
  • Ship Disadvantages: Cost, limited destinations, schedule-dependent
  • Foot Advantages: Direct routes, flexibility, no cost
  • Foot Disadvantages: Slower (15-30 mi/day), wilderness hazards, requires supplies

Route Planning

  • Study the map to understand geography
  • Consider distance vs. danger
  • Check for intermediate towns for resupply
  • Balance speed vs. cost
  • Account for seasonal conditions

Party Leadership

Travel Type Best Leader Key Skills
Wilderness Nature specialist Survival, Orientation, Track, Animal Lore
Ship Survival specialist Survival, Social skills
Road Varied Perception, Danger Sense
